Beluga whale (Delphinapterus leucas)

Photo: Vicki Beaver, Alaska Fisheries Science Center, NOAA Fisheries Service.

Beluga whales are adapted to ecosystems dominated by sea ice and rely on estuaries and lagoons especially for feeding and calving, and also rely on loose annual pack ice, leads and polynyas, and shallow continental shelf waters. They prefer shallow water in general but are known to roam to deep waters as well. These toothed whales feed on several species of fish and occasionally octopus, shrimps, crabs, mussels and clams.

Kasegaluk Lagoon and Kotzebue Sound are high concentration areas. Key habitats include feeding, molting and calving areas like Norton Sound, Ledyard bay, Kasegaluk Lagoon, Smith Bay, and Mackenzie Bay. There are three stocks of beluga whales inhabiting at least partly in the IAM area: the Eastern Beaufort Sea stock (estimated population size 32,000), the Eastern Chukchi Sea stock (3,700) and the Eastern Bering Sea stock (15,000).

Conservation status: Near Threatened (IUCN) and protected by the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) which allows harvesting only for Alaska Native subsistence uses. Belugas are an important food resource for the Alaska Natives.

Bowhead whale (Balaena mysticetus)

Photo: Amelia Brower, Alaska Fisheries Science Center, NOAA Fisheries Service.

Bowhead whales are ice-associated species and they prefer areas with dense annual pack ice, leads or polynyas, and continental shelf waters. They can live at least 150 years and mainly feed on zooplankton that they filter from the water.

Key habitats include feeding areas around the Chukchi and Beaufort sea coasts, spring migration high concentration areas like St. Lawrence Island and Barrow, and the quiet zone following the coast where Alaska Natives traditionally hunt. Bering Strait is a key migration habitat and bottleneck in spring and fall. The global population size of bowhead whales has risen up to 9,000 to 10,000 individuals from being overharvested in the beginning of 1900s, and the population trend is increasing. They are the only baleen whales not migrating to temperate or tropical waters.

Conservation status: Least Concern (IUCN) and protected by the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) which allows harvesting only for Alaska Native subsistence uses. Bowhead whales are an important food resource for the Alaska Natives.

Smith, M.A. 2010. Arctic Marine Synthesis: Atlas of the Chukchi and Beaufort Sea. Audubon Alaska and Oceana: Anchorage.

Gray whale (Eschrichtius robustus)

Photo: Merrill Cosho, Alaska Fisheries Science Center, NOAA Fisheries Service.

Gray whales are the only baleen whales feeding primarily on benthic prey by filtering zooplankton from the seafloor. They spend their summers feeding in water less than 200 m deep.

Key habitats include waters around St. Lawrence Island, the Chukotka Peninsula and the Kotzebue Sound. They are found in high concentrations in the Chirikov Basin in bering Sea. The population size of the gray whales that migrate to Alaska’s Arctic is estimated around 18,000 with population trend increasing and recovering from overharvesting. Gray whales migrate seasonally to the IAM study area from Mexico totalling 16,000 km every trip.

Conservation status: Least Concern (IUCN and protected by the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) which allows harvesting only for Alaska Native subsistence uses.

Pacific walrus (Odobenus rosmarus divergens) Pacific walrus is a long-lived pinniped that forages benthic invertebrates and relies on sea ice its whole life. As it uses dense, large ice floes for resting, birthing and nursing their existence is critical for its survival. Walrus follow the sea ice edge as it migrates north in spring and south in fall. The key habitats are located at the Hanna and Herald shoals, mating areas southwest from St. Lawrence Island and migration routes. The population size is estimated around 201,000 to 234,000 but the population trend is unknown. They feed on benthic prey and rely on areas of high benthic biomass. In the winter their distribution is quite broad while spending most of the time near broken pack ice offshore. In spring bearded seals are more concentrated while migrating through Bering Strait and following the pack ice edge. They rest, pup and molt on the ice and when the summer comes they are again more widely dispersed but continue to follow the ice edge into the Chukchi and Beaufort seas. Hanna Shoal seems to be one of the key habitats for bearded seals as it diverts warm water and results in longer ice season. The areas with 70% to 90% of pack ice concentration and high benthic biomass are essential for bearded seals and hence northern Bering Sea near St. Lawrence Island and the southern Chukotka coast can be seen as key habitats. The population in Alaska is estimated around 250,000 to 300,000 but the population trend is unknown. They mainly prey on fish and invertebrates and are themselves the main prey of polar bears. Life of the ringed seals is highly associated with ice and in winter they live near open leads maintaining breathing holes through the ice. They follow the pack ice edge to the north when temperatures rise and resting, pupping and molting takes place on the ice in spring. Ringed seals also rely on lairs that they dig at pressure ridges in the ice to protect themselves and their pups from predators and severe weather. The key habitats of ringed seals include areas with more than 90% of ice concentration and generally landfast ice is preferred rather than pack ice. High concentration of ringed seals can be found at Kasegaluk Lagoon. The population in Alaska is estimated around 249,000 but the population trend is unknown. Where they occur depends on the season, ocean currents, ice and weather conditions, and the availability of seals, especially their primary food source ringed seals. Occasionally polar bears feed on other Arctic mammals, birds and plants, and if available, marine mammal carcasses. The sea ice helps polar bears in hunting and feeding, in seeking mates and breeding, for movement to terrestrial maternity denning areas and sometimes denning itself, and for long-distance movements. Bears follow the ice edge seasonally and are most common near coastlines and the southern extent of the ice pack. The key habitat areas include critical denning areas along the Beaufort Seas coast from Barrow to the Canadian border, especially the Arctic National Wildlife Refuge coastal plain, and critical feeding habitat proposed by the U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS) located from the Beaufort sea shoreline to the continental shelf break westward towards Russia. The current population in Chukchi and Bering Sea is estimated, although not reliably, around 2,000. The population in southern Beaufort Sea is estimated around 1,500 individuals. Conservation status: Vulnerable (IUCN) and protected by the Marine Mammal Protection Act (MMPA) which allows harvesting only for Alaska Native subsistence uses. Smith, M.A. 2010.
